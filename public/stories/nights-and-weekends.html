How a cul-de-sac game night became a company

Nights and Weekends
Will Never Be the Same.

For 25 years, my days have belonged to other people's companies — advising them, scaling them, fixing them. This year, my nights and weekends went somewhere new: building one of my own.

As told by Tim Teagle · Co-Founder & CEO
7 min read

It's a game company. Our first product is a party game called Letter Me This! — born on a cul-de-sac in Southern California, built with neighbors who became friends who became business partners. And the title of this story is the whole idea, twice over: it's what happened to my calendar the day we decided to do this for real — and it's exactly what we want the game to do to yours.

In this story
  1. Part One — The Kitchen Table.  How a friend's spark at game night became a real company (and why the best ideas don't come from boardrooms).
  2. Part Two — Every Hat at Once.  What a "tiny" company actually requires — and the lessons that only show up when it's your own money, your own name, and your friends' trust on the line.
  3. Part Three — The New Leverage.  How AI tools changed what one person can build — and why your kitchen-table idea just got a lot more possible.

Let's start where it started: at the table.

1

The Kitchen Table

Nicholas Street is a cul-de-sac in Southern California where something kind of rare happened: neighbors became friends, families became regulars at each other's tables, and game nights became a standing tradition.

On one of those nights, my friend Jami came up with a game. Letter Me This! was her brainchild — a genuinely original idea where your friends describe you in a few fast, funny words and the whole room ends up laughing (and occasionally arguing) about who you really are. "Roll. Write. Reveal. Laugh." She had the spark. What she didn't have was a sense of what to do with it.

I did. After three decades in business — 25 of those years at a firm whose entire purpose is helping companies grow, scale, and optimize how they operate — I've sat on the advising side of the table for a living. I've helped others stand up operations, fix supply chains, sharpen their go-to-market. Good work. But there's a particular itch that never quite gets scratched when the company you're building is always someone else's.

So when I looked at Jami's idea, I saw something unique — not just a fun night, but a real product and a real business hiding inside it. I proposed something you don't get to do very often: that a few friends become business partners. We named the company after the street where it all started, and we got to work.

"A crazy kitchen-table idea. The best ones usually are."

The scale is, honestly, tiny — and that's exactly the point.

2

Every Hat at Once

Here's what I didn't fully appreciate until I did it myself: a "tiny" company still needs every function a big one does. Over the last several months I've gotten to personally build the things I've spent a career advising others on.

Every one of those was a discipline I "knew." Doing them — with my own money, my own name, and my friends' trust on the line — taught me things advising never could.

Every hat I wore
  1. Stood up the business — LLC, registrations, tax setup, and insurance.
  2. Built the brand and the website from scratch — a real storefront with a story to tell.
  3. Designed the product itself — the dice, the rules, the name, the voice.
  4. Designed the packaging — built to professional print spec.
  5. Developed and manufactured it — components, economics, samples in hand.
  6. Set up the supply chain — packaging, mailers, and the fulfillment model behind them.
  7. Protected the work — copyright and trademark filings on the game, the name, and our mascot.
  8. Launched a social presence and a pre-launch party for real players.
  9. Built the online buying platform — and got the game onto Amazon.

The lessons live in the pivots

The best idea we had was an accident. Our packaging is a paper canister — and somewhere along the way we realized it could double as the storage inside the game. One object, two jobs. (We also learned the hard way that it is not the shipper — so we sorted out mailer boxes too. Reality has opinions.)

Don't settle for the first answer. Finding the right manufacturing partner took patience — we kept looking until we found one whose quality and samples we genuinely loved. And when it came time for insurance, the first quote wasn't the price either: the same coverage, shopped properly, came in at a third of it. The first answer is a starting point, not a decision.

Start small on purpose. Rather than betting big, we're launching with a deliberate first run — a real-world test before we commit to scale. Prove it, then pour it on.

The box is the source of truth. We rewrote the rules more than once — then made one rule for ourselves: the production box is canon, and the website, the insert, the listing, and our AI game master all have to match it. Small inconsistencies are where trust quietly leaks out of a product.

3

The New Leverage

Here's the honest version: I handled most of the build myself — not because I'm some one-person army, but because AI tools have quietly changed what a single person can get done. I'm not a designer or a developer. But paired with these tools, I was able to stand up a real marketing website, produce print-ready packaging, work through a mountain of back-office paperwork, build a companion dice-roller app and printable score sheets — and even create Nickie, an AI Game Master built into the product itself, who can answer rules questions and settle a mid-game debate about whether that word really counts.

My favorite build of the whole project is the one no customer will ever see. We built the website, wired it to a real payment processor, then connected the entire chain behind it — so that when someone buys a game, the payment clears, the order flows through to shipping, and a little thermal printer by the window wakes up and prints the mailing label. Checkout to label, start to finish, nobody touches a keyboard.

"The first time we watched a test order come out of that printer, it felt like the company had a heartbeat."

Nickie taught me the most 2026 lesson of the whole project: the Copyright Office registers human authorship, not machine output. She's registrable because of how she was made — a fully specified character designed by a person, with AI as the rendering tool under direction. The idea was the authorship; the tool was just the tool.

Thirty-five years of business instinct, finally aimed at my own company — paired with tools that fill in the skills I don't have. That pairing is new, and it's genuinely leveling. It's why a project this small can punch above its size, and why I think a lot more people are about to be able to build the thing they've always wanted to build.

Now we're days from launch. The first production run gets assembled the way the idea arrived: friends around long tables on a Saturday — the same partners, the same families — stuffing canisters, stamping the batch code, and laughing at our own game while we build it. Batch 001.

Jami's idea, made real, built with dear friends who became business partners, chasing one specific feeling: that moment at the table when someone says something about their best friend that makes the whole room lose it, the kind of moment people bring up for years.

After 35 years of helping other people build their businesses, I finally get to build one of my own — small, scrappy, deeply personal, and more fun than anything I've done in a long time.

If you've been sitting on your own kitchen-table idea — or you know someone who has one and doesn't yet know what to do with it — this is me telling you: the tools have never been better, and the right time is closer than you think. Your nights and weekends will never be the same. Ours won't be either.
